Post-op care

In the hospital

Expect a 2–3 day hospital stay. Here’s what happens during that time.

  • The head wrap and nasal trumpets are removed 24 hours after surgery.
  • A sore throat from the endotracheal tube is normal — it fades in a day or two.
  • A physical therapist visits daily. The goal is 6 short walks around the floor per day before discharge.
  • Nursing staff will help me get out of bed — never let me try alone.
  • A registered dietician visits daily to monitor intake. Everything goes through the squeeze bottle.

Showers

  • Nursing staff must assist with every shower.
  • Face away from the shower head at all times.
  • Remain seated throughout.

Ice

  • 30 minutes on, 30 minutes off — continuously for pain and swelling management.

Going home

Discharge requires all of the following:

  • Someone must be at the hospital at discharge and able to take care of me at home.
  • Pain score below 5 on oral medications.
  • At least 1–2L of fluid per day.
  • At least 60–80g of protein per day (the meal plan targets 90g+).
  • Adequate mobility — 6 walks around the floor per day.
  • Adequate voiding.

Dr. Movahed’s office will call 1–3 days before surgery to confirm the arrival time at the hospital.

Daily care at home

Ice

30 minutes on, 30 minutes off. Continue for swelling and pain management through the first days home.

Sleep position

  • Propped up at 35–45° for the first month — use a wedge pillow or recliner.
  • After 1 month: can sleep flat on back.
  • After 1.5 months: can sleep on side.

Nutrition

  • All nutrition through a squeeze bottle for the first 2 weeks — no exceptions.
  • Track protein on the whiteboard after every feeding. Minimum 90g per day.
  • If below 90g by 9:00 PM, the nightcap is not optional.

Oral hygiene

  • After every meal: warm salt water rinse first, then Peridex.
  • Dr. Movahed’s staff brushes my teeth during post-op appointments and changes rubber bands daily.
  • No Waterpik for 2 months after surgery.

Call immediately if I have any of the following:

  • Sudden increasing swelling after it had been going down.
  • Fever.
  • Difficulty breathing.
  • Persistent bleeding that won’t stop.
  • Foul taste, foul smell, or visible pus.
  • New bite shift or hardware poking through tissue.
  • Severe pain not controlled by medications.
